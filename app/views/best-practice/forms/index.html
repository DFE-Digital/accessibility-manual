Guidance for accessible forms

Accessible forms ensure that all our users can access, navigate, and submit forms without encountering barriers. We must create forms which are clear and easy to understand while also ensuring they are accessible for anyone with a disability.

Instructions, requirements, errors, and information provided to users' needs to be clear and unambiguous which will provide a better user experience and reduce mistakes.

WCAG criteria related to forms

Many of the web content accessibility guidelines (WCAG) criteria relate to forms. This means that as well as creating a usable experience for our users, we also need to create accessible forms to ensure the department are meeting their legislative requirements.

Select the criteria to view more details and how to test your product meets the specific success requirements.

1.3.5 Identify Input Purpose (AA)

Make sure each form field has a clear purpose that assistive technologies can recognise, like fields for name, email, or address.

2.4.6 Headings and Labels (AA)

Use headings and labels that clearly describe what each section or field is about.

3.3.1 Error Identification (A)

If there's a mistake in a form field, clearly indicate which field is wrong and explain the error.

3.3.2 Labels or Instructions (A)

Provide labels or instructions to help users understand what they need to enter in each form field.

3.3.3 Error Suggestion (AA)

If there's a mistake, offer helpful suggestions on how to fix it.

3.3.4 Error Prevention (Legal, Financial, Data) (AA)

For forms that involve legal, financial, or important data, make sure users can review and correct their information before final submission.

4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A)

Ensure all form elements (like text fields, checkboxes, and buttons) have clear names and roles that can be identified by assistive technologies.
