! Warning You are breaking the law if your website or app does not have an accurate accessibility statement.

As a public body we are required by law to publish an accessibility statement on every website and app we publish.

Important:

You must publish an accessibility statement on your website before people start using it.

Follow the standard

Within the Department for Education, we have implemented a standard to ensure websites publish and maintain an accurate accessibility statements.

The standard outlines that statements must:

  • use the correct legal wording by using a statement template
  • declare the correct level of conformance
  • publish details of any issues and what is being done to resolve them
  • give people information about how to get help with an accessibility issue or request information in another format
  • provide details of any audits or testing that have been carried out
  • be available on all pages in the footer, titled “Accessibility”, for websites
  • be available at the point of download or in app guidance, for apps

You can meet these requirements by following the standard for publishing and maintaining accessibility statements which all services in DfE must comply with.

Compliance with this standard

Meeting this standard will ensure we are complying with our legal obligations and supporting people who might encounter difficulties using our websites and services.

Your website must report compliance with this standard on the Service health check hub (DfE Intranet).

The DesignOps team will also monitor and carry out audits of DfE websites to check compliance with the standard. The standard will also be assured through service assessments

If you do not follow these standards, the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) could take enforcement action against the department. You will also be preventing people with access needs from being able to use the website. This could harm our reputation and result in fines or other penalties.

How statements help people using our websites

In the UK, 1 in 5 people have a disability - this could be visual, hearing, motor (affecting fine movement) or cognitive (affecting memory and thinking). Many more people have temportary or situational impairments so it's important to ensure our websites are accessible as possible for all.

You can use the how many people tool in the DfE design manual to help you understand the range of prevelance of different types of disability in the population.

It's important statements accurately reflect known accessibility issues in a website.

Detailing the issues help people understand the level of accessibility they can expect and allows users to find alternative ways of doing what they need to if faced with an accessibility issue.

The statement must also provide information for people to get help with an accessibility issue or request information in another format.

Using the correct statement template will help you meet all these requirements in a consistent way.
